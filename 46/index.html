Chinese Language for Philly Public Schools
Coordinator: Queen Village Neighbors Association @ Queen Village Neighbors Association
Enabling Middle-Schoolers at McCall, Meredith and Nebinger Public Schools to speak Mandarin.

Chinese Language Project for Philadelphia Public Schools

This project will begin by making available instruction in Chinese (Mandarin) at 6th grade level this Fall in three diverse Center City Public Schools. In subsequent years, the plan is to add one grade of language classes each year until at least grades 6 through 8 have the option of such a program. If this initiative can be proven successful in these initial locations, further roll-out may be on a school-by-school basis, or system-wide. The project has been formed by a collaborative group including the respective Principals, Home & School and Neighborhood associations, as well as other interested entities.

We need to raise a minimum of $15,000 for the ﬁrst year. However, if we can raise $45,000, that will ensure that we can offer classes for both this year and sessions for 6th and 7th grades next year. $90,000 will ensure that the program runs for at least three years.


The schools involved

McCall, which has a signiﬁcant population of native mandarin speakers and greatly valued the language program it had in place before losing funding for this program. A great resource for the international city that Philadelphia is increasingly becoming; it has a massive catchment area, which means that even though it’s located at 5th and Pine, it’s the local school for Chinatown. McCall’s principal, Carol Domb, is an important voice in driving and focusing our efforts in Mandarin. 49.6% economically disadvantaged students.

Meredith, very much a local school in lower grades, with students largely coming from its quite small geographic catchment in the Queen Village and Bella Vista neighborhoods of South Philly, but openings in middle-school grades make this a high demand choice for kids from all over the city at that age. Meredith’s principal, Cindy Farlino, was the inspiration for this project, when she highlighted language studies as a vital missing piece in local middle schools. 49.1% economically disadvantaged students.

Nebinger, is a rising school, whose principal, Ralph Burnley was awarded the Lindback Award last year, located deeper in South Philadelphia. It’s the only public elementary school in Philadelphia with resources for autism-spectrum students, with a great reputation in this and other areas. 85.3% economically disadvantaged students

Objective

To implement an effective program which provides a functional model for language instruction at Philadelphia Public K-8 Schools. Foreign language education has broad beneﬁts in enabling more positive academic outcomes in successful testing in all areas, while global competency is a vital element of modern education. The complete lack of formal language instruction in this city’s public K-8 schools isolates students in both educational system and worldview. In addition, this program may well provide a path for accessing and implementing instructional resources in other missing educational areas, beyond language, at Philadelphia Public Schools.

Tactics

This program is focused on ensuring that each student has the opportunity participate in two in-school Chinese classes each week. While some Philadelphia public Elementary Schools currently facilitate “out of school” lessons, after school hours, these require payment and are not speciﬁcally overseen by the school’s administration. The classes envisioned by this program will be taught by foreign graduate students enrolled in Philadelphia universities, using a curriculum formed by the Main Line Chinese Cultural Center, a non-proﬁt entity with proven success in this model, that will also administer the program. The respective classes will be overseen by teachers who are fully qualiﬁed employees of the Philadelphia School District, and success vetted and facilitated by active and involved principals. A special and important aspect of this program is that it works with the public school system to add strength to the endemic institutions of learning rather than attempting to bypass public schools or existing teacher infrastructure. In the current environment, where a lack of available funds makes every Philadelphia Public School a disadvantaged school, new models must be found for adding resources that cannot be taken away. It is our hope that the project described here may be a successful model for doing exactly this.

Beneﬁts of foreign language study (National Council of State Supervisors for Languages)
  • Foreign language learners consistently outperform control groups in core subject areas on standardized tests, often signiﬁcantly. (Armstrong & Rogers 1997, Saunders 1998, Masciantonio 1977, Rafferty 1986, Andrade, Kretschmer & Kretschmer 1989)
  • Children of color, children from economically disadvantaged backgrounds, and English Language Learners make the greatest proportionate achievement gains from foreign language study. (Curtain & Dahlberg 2004)
  • Foreign language study can help to alter the trajectory for children of average intelligence and narrow the achievement gap. (Garﬁnkel & Tabor 1991)
  • Students of foreign languages tend to score higher on standardized tests. Results from the Scholastic Aptitude Test (SAT) show that students who had studied a foreign language for 4 or more years outscored other students on the verbal and math portions of the test. (College Board 2003)
Chinese is chosen as the initial language for its multiple unique beneﬁts:
  • Differentiating and of high perceived value in the current world economy
  • Available at the magnet High Schools to which high achieving students will apply
  • Chinese students represent a signiﬁcant population at one of the target schools
  • Additional beneﬁt in addressing known social/racial conﬂicts in this city
